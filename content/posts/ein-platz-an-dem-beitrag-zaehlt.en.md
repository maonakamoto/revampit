Beneath our logo stand three words: **menschen, hardware, knowhow**. Two of them — people and know-how — are not about devices at all, but about persons: about what they can do, learn, and become. That is no accident. A used laptop that others wrote off long ago works again after a repair — and, through our [marketplace](/marketplace), reaches someone who needs it. That is the visible half of our mission. The other half is about people who have fallen out of working life, or never properly found their way into it, and who find with us a place where their contribution counts.

This is not a by-product. Anyone who tests, repairs, catalogues, and passes on devices at Revamp-IT is doing real work of real value. That is exactly why this place is so well suited to a return to work: there is no occupational therapy here, but a workshop, a marketplace, [workshops](/workshops), and [IT help](/it-hilfe) — an organisation that depends on the work being done. Our [work-reintegration programme](/get-involved/work-reintegration) builds precisely on that: structured work experience, individual support, a pace that fits the person.

## Why returning to work is hard

The labour market filters harshly. A gap in the CV, a long illness, an exhaustion, a break in one's biography — and suddenly you can no longer get past the first hurdle. The problem is rarely a lack of ability. It is the missing proof that you contribute reliably, and often enough the lost belief that you can still do it at all.

What people in this situation need is well known: a structure that supports without overwhelming. Tasks that you actually complete and whose results you can see. A team that treats you as a colleague and not as a case. And — quietly, but decisively — evidence. Something that shows: *I made that. I can do that. I learned that.*

It is exactly at this point that a workshop question becomes a portal question. Because a large part of what carries a return to work is the visibility of contribution and development — and visibility is something software does well.

## What the portal can already do today

The portal's collaboration area was not designed as a social-work tool. But precisely because it reflects normal, honest team work, it works for the return to work. Four building blocks are productive today:

**Personal profiles.** Every person who works here has a profile with skills, interests, goals, strengths, and areas for development — not as a rigid personnel file, but as a growing description of what someone can do and where he or she wants to go. A profile already brings together everything a person has contributed today: their listings on the [marketplace](/marketplace), the services and [IT help](/it-hilfe) they offer, workshops they give, and posts they have written. Skills are keywords that accumulate over time. Goals, strengths, and areas for development live in a separate "Development" view that each person maintains for themselves. On top of that comes a "current focus": a sentence about what you are working on right now, which becomes visible to the team on the "Who does what" board. Someone just starting out might have three entries; after half a year there are twelve. For the person, this curve is often the first tangible sign that things are moving forward.

**Team profiles.** In the portal, teams are self-standing units with their own goals and metrics. A team has a list of goals with status and a handful of metrics against which it measures its progress. For someone who has stood on the outside for a long time, belonging to such a visible unit is more than organisation — it is an "I belong". You are not *at* Revamp-IT, you are *in* the workshop team, and the team has a goal you contribute to.

**Tasks.** Tasks can be assigned, given due dates, and ticked off. That sounds trivial and is not: a completed, assigned task is a small, finished proof of reliability. Many small proofs add up over time into a pattern you can rely on — the person themselves first of all.

**Decisions and votes.** In the decisions area, proposals are discussed and then voted on — with a clear flow from draft through discussion to the vote, and with different methods depending on the question, from a simple yes/no to consent, where it is not about the majority but about workable agreement. Every voice counts equally. For someone who has long felt unasked, a voice that weighs as much as any other is a powerful signal: *Your view is part of the decision.*

On top of this come **deliverables** and **minutes** — reports, presentations, and notes that record what a team produces and discusses. And on the personnel side, the portal also maps the return to work formally: there is a dedicated track for returners with onboarding building blocks such as a reintegration conversation and an assigned contact person for the first weeks.

## The profile as a tool for self-confidence

The most important of these building blocks is the personal profile — not because of the technology, but because of the effect.

A profile that places skills, interests, and goals side by side does three things at once. It makes **ability visible**: what before was only a vague "I can sort of handle computers" becomes a concrete list — hardware diagnostics, data erasure, customer conversation, cataloguing. It makes **development visible**: the list grows, and growth that you can see, you are more likely to believe. And it holds on to **direction**: interests and goals remind us that a person with wishes works here, not a function.

For the labour market, that is precisely the raw material that is otherwise missing. A well-kept profile is the beginning of a portfolio: an honest, verifiable answer to the question "What can you do?" Anyone who learns with us to test devices and prepare them for the [marketplace](/marketplace) also learns, along the way, things that count everywhere — technical troubleshooting, careful work by checklist, customer contact, team coordination, a feel for deadlines. The portal is the place where these skills are not lost but accumulate.

## Measuring without taking someone's measure

An organisation that means it seriously needs processes and must measure its impact. We want to know whether our approach carries: are people getting ahead? Are they finding their way back into work? That is not bureaucracy, it is responsibility — towards the people who trust us, and towards those who make our work possible.

But here lies the fine line that matters. A person returning to work must never feel *sized up*. Anyone who feels monitored and judged makes themselves small — exactly the opposite of what we want. Numbers that decide over a person crush; numbers that show a person how far they have already come lift them up.

That is why the attitude matters more than the tool. The portal should make progress visible *for the person*, not report *about the person*. The "Development" view belongs to the person themselves. Confidentiality is not an added feature but a precondition. And every metric we build must pass the question: does it strengthen this person's self-confidence — or does it merely serve our reporting needs? Empathy and impact measurement are not mutually exclusive. But the order is not negotiable: first the person, then the metric.

## A more individual approach — what we could build

As well as today's building blocks work, they are team tools that are *also used* for the return to work. The next step would be to make the individual development path a first-class citizen of the portal in its own right. Honestly: that is not built yet. But it is within reach, and it would make the work of social support noticeably easier and more effective.

| Built today | What we could build |
| --- | --- |
| Skills as a keyword list | Skills with **proficiency levels** and a history that shows growth over time |
| Goals as a free-text field | A **development plan** with milestones that you reach and tick off one by one |
| Interests as a list | Interests and wishes that suggest matching **tasks and learning steps** |
| Ticking off tasks | Tasks that belong to a **learning path** — from the simple to the demanding |
| Profile as an internal view | An **exportable portfolio** for job applications, with evidenced skills |
| Assigning a buddy manually | A quiet **support view** in which a person's progress, mood, and next steps come together |

The last row is the most important. A supporting specialist today juggles a great deal in their head and in scattered notes: where does this person stand? What was the last good step? What is the next? A confidential, protected place in the portal where a person's path comes together clearly — skills, milestones reached, open tasks, agreed goals — would relieve this work of the bookkeeping and free the view onto what matters: the person.

Such an individual approach would above all take seriously what people bring with them: their interests, their inclinations, their dreams. Not everyone who starts over with us wants to become a repair technician. Some discover cataloguing and describing, some customer contact, some logistics, some teaching in a [workshop](/workshops). A portal that holds on to this direction and suggests fitting next steps can kindle a passion — and turn "some occupation or other" into a path that belongs to the person. Paths that belong to you, you keep walking.

## Why this is part of the mission

It is the same basic idea that keeps a working laptop from ending up as electronic waste: it is a waste to write off something valuable just because the market happens not to be looking. For devices, we built a workshop. For people, we are building a place where contribution counts, becomes visible, and grows — and a portal that carries this path.

The goal is not only the return to the labour market. The goal is that a person finds something they enjoy learning and enjoy doing — and thereby becomes a sustainable, load-bearing part of their community. That is exactly where the circle closes back to **menschen, hardware, knowhow**: we do not only repair devices, we help people rebuild their know-how and their self-confidence.

Part of this is already in place: profiles, teams, tasks, votes, a dedicated track for the return to work. The next part — the individual development path as a first-class citizen, thought through with empathy and kept confidential — is the work that lies ahead of us. Anyone who would like to walk this path with us or make it possible will find the way in under [Get involved](/get-involved). It is worth it, because it starts exactly where the mission becomes most human.
