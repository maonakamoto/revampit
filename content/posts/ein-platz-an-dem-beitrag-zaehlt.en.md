Beneath our logo stand three words: **menschen, hardware, knowhow**. Two of them — people and know-how — are not about devices at all, but about persons: about what they can do, learn and become. That is no accident. A used laptop that others long ago wrote off works again after repair — and reaches someone who needs it via our [marketplace](/marketplace). That is the visible half of our mission. The other half is about people who have fallen out of working life or never properly found their way into it, and who find with us a place where their contribution counts.

![The Revamp-IT marketplace with refurbished devices, filters and CO₂ figures](/blog/showcase-revampit-marketplace-grid.png)
*The visible half: tested, refurbished devices on the marketplace — each with transparent CO₂ savings. Every one of these listings was prepared, photographed and described by a person.*

This is not a by-product. Whoever tests, repairs, catalogues and passes on devices at Revamp-IT is doing real work with real value. That is exactly why this place is so well suited to reintegration: there is no occupational therapy here, but a workshop, a marketplace, [workshops](/workshops) and [IT support](/it-hilfe) — an organisation that depends on the work being done. Our [reintegration programme](/get-involved/work-reintegration) is built precisely on that: structured work experience, individual support, a pace that suits the person.

## Why Returning to Work Is Hard — and What the Research Says

The labour market filters harshly. A gap in the CV, a long illness, an exhaustion, a break in the biography — and suddenly you can no longer get past the first hurdle. The problem is rarely a lack of ability. It is the lack of proof that one contributes reliably, and often enough the lost belief that one is still able to at all.

That unemployment is not merely a financial but a psychological problem is well documented. The large meta-analysis by Karsten Paul and Klaus Moser evaluated 237 cross-sectional and 87 longitudinal studies and found a clear connection: among the unemployed, an average of **34% showed psychological complaints, among the employed only 16%**. Crucially, the longitudinal data clarify the direction — it is not merely that psychologically strained people lose their jobs more often; the loss of work itself worsens mental health, and the return to work improves it again (Paul & Moser, *[Unemployment impairs mental health: Meta-analyses](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0001879109000037)*, Journal of Vocational Behavior, 2009). Reintegration is therefore not only economically sensible; it is, in the literal sense, healing.

Why this is so was described by the social psychologist Marie Jahoda as early as 1982. Besides the wage, work pays out five "latent" benefits that we barely notice as long as we have them: a **time structure** for the day, **social contacts** outside the family, a **shared goal** beyond one's own person, **status and identity**, and simply regular **activity**. When work falls away, all five fall away — and it is precisely these that the people who come to us often miss more than the money.

That this is not merely an old theory is confirmed by newer data from the German-speaking world. An analysis of the German panel "Labour Market and Social Security" with over 9,300 respondents found that the unemployed score worse on all five latent functions than the employed — and indeed even compared to people in small part-time jobs, where the wage barely matters (Bähr & Collischon, *[Erwerbsarbeit erfüllt wichtige psychologische Funktionen](https://iab-forum.de/erwerbsarbeit-erfuellt-wichtige-psychologische-funktionen/)*, IAB-Forum, 2022). So it really is the work itself, not just the income, that is missing.

What people in this situation need follows directly from this: a structure that carries without overwhelming. Tasks that one really completes and whose results one can see. A team that treats one as a colleague and not as a case. And — quietly, but decisively — proof. Something that shows: *I made that. I can do that. I learned that.*

It is exactly at this point that a workshop question becomes a portal question. For a large part of what carries reintegration is the visibility of contribution and development — and visibility is something software does well.

## What the Portal Can Already Do Today

The collaboration section of the portal was not designed as a social-work tool. But precisely because it maps normal, honest teamwork, it works for reintegration. Four building blocks are productive today:

**Personal profiles.** Every contributing person has a profile with skills, interests, goals, strengths and areas for development — not as a rigid personnel file, but as a growing description of what someone can do and where they want to go. A profile today already bundles everything a person has contributed: their listings on the [marketplace](/marketplace), the services and [IT support](/it-hilfe) they offer, workshops they give, and articles they have written.

![Public profile of a contributing person with listings and articles](/blog/showcase-revampit-profile.png)
*A public profile bundles everything a person has contributed — here six listings and eleven articles, each with visible impact. Individual small acts become a documentable trail.*

Skills are keywords that accumulate over time. Goals, strengths and areas for development stand in a separate "Development" view that each person maintains for themselves. Added to this is a "current focus": a sentence about what one is working on right now, which becomes visible to the team on the "Who does what" board. Someone just starting out may have three entries; after half a year there are twelve. For the person, this curve is often the first tangible sign that things are moving forward.

**Team profiles.** In the portal, teams are independent units with their own goals and metrics. A team has a list of goals with statuses and a handful of metrics against which it measures its progress. For someone who stood outside for a long time, belonging to such a visible unit is more than organisation — it is an "I belong here". You are not *at* Revamp-IT, you are *in* the workshop team, and the team has a goal you contribute to. That is exactly the third of Jahoda's latent benefits — the shared goal beyond one's own person — cast in software.

![The team overview in the admin portal with eight teams, their responsibilities and leads](/blog/showcase-revampit-admin-teams.png)
*The teams in the portal — from the repair team to the shop team to the warehouse team, each with a clear responsibility and a handful of members. Someone joining sees at a glance where they belong and what their team stands for. On the left, the "Collaboration" group, where tasks, deliverables and minutes converge.*

**Tasks.** Tasks can be assigned, given due dates and ticked off. That sounds trivial and it is not: a completed, assigned task is a small, finished proof of reliability. Many small proofs form, over time, a pattern one can rely on — the person themselves first.

**Decisions and votes.** In the decisions area, proposals are discussed and then voted on — with a clear flow from draft through discussion to vote, and with different methods depending on the question, from a simple yes/no to consent, where it is not about the majority but about workable agreement. Every vote counts equally. For someone who has long felt unasked, a vote that carries the same weight as any other is a powerful signal: *Your view is part of the decision.*

![The decisions area in the admin portal with an ongoing proposal up for a vote](/blog/showcase-revampit-admin-decisions.png)
*An ongoing proposal in the decisions area: title, type, voting method and status at a glance. The flow from draft to vote is equally visible to everyone — participation here is not a promise but a feature.*

Added to this are **deliverables** and **minutes** — reports, presentations and notes that record what a team produces and discusses. And on the personnel side, the portal also maps reintegration formally: there is a dedicated track for returners with onboarding building blocks such as a reintegration interview and an assigned contact person for the first weeks.

![The minutes overview with meeting notes and the AI-assisted review queue that turns them into tasks](/blog/showcase-revampit-admin-protocols.png)
*Minutes record what was discussed — and a review view turns them into concrete tasks and decisions, instead of letting them fade away in notes. This turns "we talked at some point" into a traceable thread everyone can keep working on.*

## The Profile as a Tool for Self-Confidence

The most important of these building blocks is the personal profile — not because of the technology, but because of its effect. And here a look at the research is worthwhile once more.

The psychologist Albert Bandura showed that self-efficacy — the belief that one can actually master a thing — draws its strongest source from **one's own experiences of success**. Not encouragement, not observation, not positive thinking, but *having achieved something oneself* changes conviction most durably (Bandura, *[Self-efficacy: Toward a unifying theory of behavioral change](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.84.2.191)*, Psychological Review, 1977). That is exactly what a well-kept profile does: it is a record of one's own experiences of success. Every catalogued device, every listing, every completed task is a small, documented proof — and proofs you see in black and white work more strongly than any "you can do it".

A profile that places skills, interests and goals side by side does three things at once. It makes **ability visible**: what was previously only a vague "I'm somehow good with computers" becomes a concrete list — hardware diagnostics, data erasure, customer conversation, cataloguing. It makes **development visible**: the list grows, and growth you can see is more readily believed. And it holds onto **direction**: interests and goals remind us that a person with wishes works here, not a function.

For the labour market this is exactly the raw material that is otherwise missing. A well-kept profile is the beginning of a portfolio: an honest, documentable answer to the question "What can you do?" Whoever learns with us to test devices and prepare them for the [marketplace](/marketplace) learns, along the way, things that count everywhere — technical troubleshooting, careful work by checklist, customer contact, team coordination, a sense for deadlines. The portal is the place where these skills are not lost but accrue.

## Real Work That Carries: From Repair to Handover

The experiences of success that a profile records do not arise in a vacuum. They arise from real jobs. In [IT support](/it-hilfe), for instance, people with a repair need meet technicians who can help — a two-sided network in which supply and demand find each other.

![IT support: the repair and technician network](/blog/showcase-revampit-it-hilfe.png)

The difference from a sheltered practice situation is decisive. A customer whose laptop is running again does not thank you for an exercise, but for a solved worry. A buyer who purchases a tested device on the marketplace relies on the testing being sound. It is this seriousness that carries self-confidence: you are needed, not merely occupied.

## A Programme That Takes "Place First, Then Train" Seriously

How best to support people back into work is surprisingly well researched. The most effective approach for people with difficult access to the labour market is called **Individual Placement and Support (IPS)** — in German most closely "supported employment". Its basic principle reverses the old order: not to practise for a long time first in sheltered workshops and then, at some point, move into real work ("train then place"), but to be in real work as early as possible and to apply the support *there* ("place then train").

The evidence for this is clear. An international meta-analysis of 17 randomised controlled trials found that people with IPS support were **about 2.4 times more likely** to find regular employment than people in classical vocational rehabilitation (Modini et al., *[Supported employment for people with severe mental illness](https://doi.org/10.1192/bjp.bp.115.165092)*, British Journal of Psychiatry, 2016; overview at the [IPS Employment Center](https://ipsworks.org/index.php/evidence-for-ips/)). The same analysis is honest about its limitation: in economically weak times the advantage shrinks, because IPS too depends on real, available positions. All the more valuable, then, is an organisation that itself continuously has real work to hand out.

For Switzerland this effect is directly documented — and indeed here in Zurich. In a randomised study by the Psychiatric University Hospital Zurich with 250 disability pensioners with a mental illness, within two years **32%** of the IPS-supported group found a position in the primary labour market — compared to only **12%** in the classically supervised control group (Viering et al., *[Supported Employment for the Reintegration of Disability Pensioners with Mental Illnesses](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4611964/)*, Frontiers in Public Health, 2015). A six-year follow-up by the same research group is remarkably honest about it: the initially clear lead (**40% versus 28%** after two years) evens out over the years (**36% versus 33%** after six years) (Pichler et al., *[Long-Term Effects of the Individual Placement and Support Intervention](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8546221/)*, Frontiers in Psychiatry, 2021). The finding is a pointer we take seriously: a good start alone is not enough — the support must remain viable so that the reintegration holds.

Our [reintegration programme](/get-involved/work-reintegration) is deliberately built along this logic: real tasks from the start, a supportive team around it, individual guidance and a pace oriented to the person. With this we are a social enterprise in the true sense — and that this model works has also been established by the federal government: a foundational report commissioned by the Federal Social Insurance Office, based on around 300 Swiss social enterprises, concludes that they make "an important contribution to the social and occupational integration" of people who have become distant from the labour market (Ferrari et al., *[Sozialfirmen in der Schweiz](https://www.bsv.admin.ch/bsv/de/home/publikationen-und-service/medieninformationen/nsb-anzeigeseite.msg-id-64121.html)*, BSV, 2016). Only our social enterprise additionally prevents electronic waste.

## Measuring Without Surveilling

An organisation that means it seriously needs processes and must measure its impact. We want to know whether our approach works: Do people move forward? Do they find their way back into work? That is not bureaucracy, that is responsibility — towards the people who trust us, and towards those who make our work possible. We apply the same attitude to our ecological impact: every figure is disclosed and derived, nothing is merely claimed.

![The impact page "Our measurable impact" with the section on the e-waste crisis](/blog/showcase-revampit-impact.png)
*Transparency as a principle: on the impact page we disclose figures and their derivation — for the environment as for the people.*

But here lies the fine line that matters. A person in reintegration must never feel *surveilled*. Whoever has the feeling of being monitored and evaluated makes themselves small — exactly the opposite of what we want. Numbers that decide over a person crush; numbers that show a person how far they have already come lift them up.

That is why the attitude matters more than the tool. The portal should make progress visible *for the person*, not report *about the person*. The "Development" view belongs to the person themselves. Confidentiality is not an add-on feature but a precondition. And every metric we build must pass the question: Does it strengthen this person's self-confidence — or does it merely serve our reporting need? Empathy and impact measurement are not mutually exclusive. But the order is non-negotiable: first the person, then the metric.

## Publicly Co-Financed — Therefore Accountable

Revamp-IT does not sustain itself solely from what the workshop and marketplace earn. Part of our work is co-supported by the City of Zurich's [KlimUp](https://www.stadt-zuerich.ch/de/umwelt-und-energie/klima/beratung-und-foerderung/klimup.html) funding programme — Zurich tax money that the city deploys purposefully for climate protection and intelligent use of resources. This money flows nominally for the ecological side of our mission — every rescued laptop is avoided electronic waste. But public funds fundamentally oblige: whoever works with tax money owes the public transparency about what comes of it — and indeed across the whole mission, not only the part the money was meant for.

That is why we resolve to measure our impact seriously — the ecological as well as the social — and to disclose it. Not because a regulation demands it, but because it is the honest way of dealing with trust. The ecological side we already disclose today on our [metrics page](/transparenz/kennzahlen), every figure with its derivation. The social side — reintegration — is harder to measure without surveilling the people. But harder does not mean impossible. It only means: more carefully.

**Two levels that must never be confused.** The *personal* level belongs to the person and to no one else. Here it is about growth that becomes visible *for them* — the growing list of skills, milestones reached, a voluntary self-assessment of how confident they feel. These numbers do not leave the "Development" view; they are a mirror, not a file. The *programme* level, by contrast, is aggregated and anonymised. Here we do not ask "How is person X doing?" but "Does our approach work?" — many individual paths become a metric in which no single person is any longer recognisable. It is precisely these numbers that may and should go outward: to the funders, to the public, to ourselves. The rule behind it is simple: **individual data lift the person up, aggregated data steer the programme.** As soon as an individual number decides over a person or compares them with others, we have crossed the line.

**What we could concretely measure.** The following selection is oriented to what the research knows to be meaningful — the transition into work (the IPS result), self-efficacy (Bandura), retention over time — and to what we can gather *without* building a surveillance apparatus:

| Metric (programme level, aggregated) | What it shows | Where the data come from |
| --- | --- | --- |
| People in reintegration per year | the reach of the programme | the personnel track in the portal |
| Transition rate into a follow-on solution | whether reintegration succeeds (regular position, training, follow-on programme) | closing record at programme end |
| Retention after 6 and 12 months | whether the transition holds, not just succeeds | voluntary follow-up survey |
| Self-efficacy before / after | whether confidence grows — the actual core | short, voluntary, validated self-assessment |
| Documented skills per person (average) | whether and how fast ability grows | the profiles — arises as a by-product |
| Completed real jobs | that it was real work, not an exercise | cataloguing, listings, IT support |
| Recommendation and satisfaction | whether the place feels right for the people | short anonymous feedback |

Deliberately *not* on this list: individual "productivity", rankings, pace comparisons between people. Such numbers may fill a report, but they damage exactly what we want to build.

**Where the portal makes the difference.** The most important point is at once technical and ethical: almost all of these metrics are a *by-product* of the work that happens in the portal anyway — no additional recording effort, no separate surveillance apparatus. When someone catalogues a device, puts up a listing or ticks off a task, the proof of "real work" arises on its own. When the skills list in the profile grows, the growth curve arises on its own. The portal only has to *tally these traces up anonymously* — each person counts once, without a name — and out of it comes a programme dashboard that can stand next to our ecological [metrics page](/transparenz/kennzahlen). Because the data come from a single source — from what is already recorded anyway — there is no double bookkeeping, no contradictory numbers and no reason to burden people with forms. Only two things must be deliberately added, and both belong first to the person: the voluntary self-assessment of self-efficacy and the follow-up survey on retention. Both are optional, both are anonymous at the programme level, and both give something back to the person before anything is reported to anyone.

This closes the circle back to the attitude from before: the portal measures by making visible what the people accomplish anyway — and translates it into accountability only once no single person is any longer recognisable. Making the impact bigger and protecting the people is no contradiction, as long as the measurement arises out of the work itself rather than alongside it.

## A More Individual Approach — What We Could Build

As well as today's building blocks work: they are team tools that are *co-used* for reintegration. The next step would be to make the individual development path its own, first-class citizen in the portal. Honestly: that is not yet built today. But it is close at hand, and it would make the work of social support noticeably easier and more effective.

| Built today | What we could build |
| --- | --- |
| Skills as a keyword list | Skills with **proficiency levels** and a history that shows growth over time |
| Goals as a free-text field | A **development plan** with milestones one reaches and ticks off individually |
| Interests as a list | Interests and wishes that suggest matching **tasks and learning steps** |
| Ticking off tasks | Tasks that belong to a **learning path** — from the simple to the demanding |
| Profile as an internal view | An **exportable portfolio** for job applications, with documented skills |
| Assigning a buddy manually | A calm **support view** in which a person's progress, mood and next steps converge |

The last row is the most important. A supporting specialist today juggles much in their head and in scattered notes: Where does this person stand? What was the last good step? What is the next? A confidential, protected place in the portal where a person's path comes together clearly — skills, milestones reached, open tasks, agreed goals — would relieve this work of the bookkeeping and free the view for what matters: the person.

The "learning path" in the middle row, by the way, also has backing in the research. Experiences of success strengthen self-confidence most when the tasks are **just the right amount of hard** — doable, but not handed out for free. A path that leads from the simple to the demanding keeps people precisely in that zone in which "I managed that" turns into genuine confidence.

Such an individual approach would above all take seriously what people bring with them: their interests, their inclinations, their dreams. Not everyone who starts again with us wants to become a repair technician. Some discover cataloguing and describing, some customer contact, some logistics, some teaching in a [workshop](/workshops). A portal that holds onto this direction and suggests matching next steps can ignite a passion — and turn "some occupation or other" into a path that belongs to the person. Paths that belong to you, you keep walking.

## Why This Belongs to the Mission

It is the same basic idea that keeps a working laptop from ending up in the electronic waste: it is a waste to write off something valuable just because the market happens not to be looking. And the waste in devices is on a vast scale: according to the UN's [Global E-waste Monitor 2024](https://ewastemonitor.info/the-global-e-waste-monitor-2024/), **62 million tonnes** of electronic waste arose worldwide in 2022 — growing five times faster than documented recycling, of which only **22.3%** is captured. Much of it is not broken, merely old, slow or unfashionable.

Switzerland collects exemplarily: in 2024 the take-back systems Swico and SENS processed around **135,200 tonnes** of old devices, with a mandated recovery rate of at least **75%** ([Technical Report 2025](https://www.fachbericht.ch/en/fachbericht-2025.html) by Swico Recycling and SENS eRecycling). Yet recycling is only the second-best solution. A life-cycle assessment by Empa shows why: for computers, laptops and smartphones the largest part of the environmental burden lies not in operation but in **manufacture** — which is why ecologically the clear greatest benefit comes from continuing to use such a device rather than melting it down and building a new one (Böni & Hischier, *[Weiter- und Wiederverwendung von elektrischen und elektronischen Geräten](https://www.dora.lib4ri.ch/empa/islandora/object/empa:18493)*, Empa, 2018). That is exactly where we come in: reuse beats recycling — for every single device.

For devices we have built a workshop. For people we are building a place where contribution counts, becomes visible and grows — and a portal that carries this path. The parallelism is not a rhetorical trick: in both cases it is about making hidden value usable again, instead of writing it off.

The goal is not only reintegration into the labour market. The goal is that a person finds something they gladly learn and gladly do — and thereby becomes a sustainable, supporting part of their community. It is exactly here that the circle closes back to **menschen, hardware, knowhow**: we do not only repair devices, we help people rebuild their know-how and their self-confidence.

Part of this already stands: profiles, teams, tasks, votes, a dedicated track for reintegration. The next part — the individual development path as a first-class citizen, conceived empathetically and kept confidential — is the work that lies before us. Whoever would like to walk this path along with us or make it possible finds the way in under [Get involved](/get-involved). It is worthwhile, because it starts exactly where the mission becomes most human.
