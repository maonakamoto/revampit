A used laptop that others wrote off long ago works again after repair — and ends up with someone who needs it. That is the visible half of our mission. The less visible half is about people: about people who have fallen out of working life or never quite found their way into it, and who find with us a place where their contribution counts.

This is not a by-product. Whoever tests, repairs, catalogues and passes on devices at Revamp-IT does real work with real value. That is precisely why this place is so well suited to reintegration: there is no occupational therapy here, but a workshop, a marketplace, an organisation that depends on the work being done. Our [work-reintegration programme](/get-involved/work-reintegration) builds on exactly that — structured work experience, individual support, a pace that fits the person.

## Why returning to work is hard

The labour market filters harshly. A gap in the CV, a longer illness, an exhaustion, a break in one's biography — and suddenly you can no longer get past the first hurdle. The problem is rarely a lack of ability. It is the missing proof that you contribute reliably, and often enough the lost belief that you are even capable of it at all.

What people in this situation need is well known: a structure that supports without overwhelming. Tasks that you actually complete and where you see the result. A team that treats you as a colleague and not as a case. And — quietly, but decisively — evidence. Something that shows: *I did that. I can do that. I learned that.*

It is at exactly this point that a workshop question becomes a portal question. Because a large part of what carries reintegration is the visibility of contribution and development — and visibility is something software does well.

## What the portal can already do today

The portal's collaboration page was not designed as a social tool. But precisely because it maps normal, honest team work, it works for reintegration. Four building blocks are productive today:

**Personal profiles.** Every staff member has a profile with skills, interests, goals, strengths and areas for development — not as a rigid personnel file, but as a growing description of what someone can do and where they want to go. Skills are keywords that fill out over time. Goals, strengths and areas for development are already there today in a dedicated "Development" view that each person can access and maintain for themselves. On top of that comes a "current focus": a sentence about what you are working on right now, which becomes visible to the team on the "Who does what" board. Someone who is just starting out perhaps has three entries; after six months there are twelve. For the person, this curve is often the first tangible sign that things are moving forward.

**Team profiles.** Teams are independent units in the portal with their own goals and metrics. A team has a list of goals with status and a handful of metrics against which it measures its progress. For someone who has stood outside for a long time, belonging to such a visible unit is more than organisation — it is an "I belong". You are not *at* Revamp-IT, you are *in* the workshop team, and the team has a goal to which you contribute.

**Tasks.** Tasks can be assigned, given due dates and ticked off. That sounds trivial and it is not: a completed, assigned task is a small, finished proof of reliability. Many small proofs add up over time to a pattern you can rely on — the person themselves first.

**Decisions and votes.** In the decisions area, proposals are discussed and then voted on — with a clear process from draft through discussion to the vote, and with different methods depending on the question, from a simple yes/no to consent, where it is not about the majority but about workable agreement. Every voice counts equally. For someone who long felt unasked, a voice that weighs as much as any other is a strong signal: *Your perspective is part of the decision.*

Added to this are **deliverables** and **minutes** — reports, presentations and notes that record what a team produces and discusses. And on the HR page, the portal also maps reintegration formally: there is a dedicated track for returners with onboarding building blocks such as a reintegration conversation and an assigned contact person for the first weeks.

## The profile as a tool for self-confidence

The most important of these building blocks is the personal profile — not because of the technology, but because of its effect.

A profile that places skills, interests and goals side by side does three things at once. It makes **ability visible**: what was previously only a diffuse "I can somehow work with computers" becomes a concrete list — hardware diagnosis, data erasure, customer conversation, cataloguing. It makes **development visible**: the list grows, and growth you can see is growth you are more likely to believe in. And it holds onto **direction**: interests and goals remind us that a person with wishes works here, not a function.

For the labour market, this is exactly the raw material that is otherwise missing. A well-kept profile is the beginning of a portfolio: an honest, verifiable answer to the question "What can you do?". Whoever learns with us to test devices and prepare them for the [marketplace](/marketplace) learns, along the way, things that count everywhere — technical troubleshooting, careful work following a checklist, customer contact, team coordination, a sense for deadlines. The portal is the place where these skills are not lost but accumulate.

## A more individual approach — what we could build

As well as today's building blocks work: they are team tools that are *also used* for reintegration. The next step would be to make the individual development path a first-class citizen of the portal in its own right. Honestly: this is not yet built today. But it is within reach, and it would make the work of social support noticeably easier and more effective.

| Built today | What we could build |
| --- | --- |
| Skills as a keyword list | Skills with **levels** and a history that shows growth over time |
| Goals as a free-text field | A **development plan** with milestones you reach and tick off one by one |
| Interests as a list | Interests and wishes that suggest matching **tasks and learning steps** |
| Ticking off tasks | Tasks that belong to a **learning path** — from the simple to the demanding |
| Profile as an internal view | An **exportable portfolio** for applications, with evidenced skills |
| Assigning a buddy manually | A calm **support view** in which a person's progress, mood and next steps come together |

The last row is the most important. A supporting professional today juggles a lot in their head and in scattered notes: Where does this person stand? What was the last good step? What is the next one? A confidential, protected place in the portal, where a person's path comes together clearly — skills, milestones reached, open tasks, agreed goals — would relieve this work of the bookkeeping and free the view to what matters: the person. Confidentiality is not an add-on feature here but a prerequisite; the dignity and privacy of the person being supported takes precedence over any evaluation.

Such an individual approach would also take seriously what people bring with them: their interests, their inclinations, their dreams. Not everyone who starts again with us wants to become a repair technician. Some discover cataloguing and describing, some customer contact, some logistics. A portal that holds onto this direction and suggests matching next steps turns "some kind of occupation" into a path that belongs to the person — and paths that belong to you are paths you keep walking.

## Why this belongs to the mission

It is the same basic idea that saves a working laptop from ending up in electronic waste: it is a waste to write off something valuable just because the market happens not to be looking. For devices, we built a workshop. For people, we are building a place where contribution counts, becomes visible and grows — and a portal that carries that path.

Part of it already stands: profiles, teams, tasks, votes, a dedicated track for reintegration. The next part — the individual development path as a first-class citizen — is the work that lies ahead of us. It is worth it, because it takes hold exactly where the mission becomes most human.
